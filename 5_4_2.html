D) The mirror bends all rays at the same angle

A) Specular reflection

C) Diffuse reflection
D) Constructive reflection
E) Backward refraction

B) The temperature of the medium
C) The index of refraction of the medium
D) The wavelength of the light
E) The frequency of the light

B) It would continue straight without bending
C) It would bend away from the normal line
D) It would reflect back into the water
E) It would diffract in all directions

A) Reflection from the barrier
B) Refraction at the opening
C) Diffraction around the edges of the opening
D) Constructive interference in the opening
E) Complete absorption by the barrier

A) Long wavelengths diffract less than short wavelengths
B) Long wavelengths diffract more than short wavelengths
C) Wavelength has no effect on diffraction
D) Diffraction only occurs with short wavelengths
E) Diffraction requires wavelengths longer than visible light

A) Destructive interference
B) Constructive interference
C) No interference (waves pass through)
D) Partial interference
E) Diffraction at the meeting point

A) The amplitudes add to produce a larger wave
B) The amplitudes cancel each other out, potentially resulting in no wave
C) The waves reflect off each other
D) The waves refract at different angles
E) The waves separate into two different waves

A) It shows that only one wave can exist at a time
B) It demonstrates that waves always repel each other
C) It explains how constructive and destructive interference patterns form from wave addition
D) It proves that interference cannot occur in nature
E) It shows waves travel in straight lines only

A) The waves are traveling in the same direction at the same speed
B) The waves have the same wavelength but different frequencies
C) The waves arrive at the same point with crests aligned with crests (and troughs with troughs)
D) The waves are separated by exactly one wavelength
E) The waves originate from different sources

B) Standing waves appear to remain stationary, formed from interference of incident and reflected waves
C) Standing waves travel faster than regular waves
D) Standing waves only occur in liquids
E) Standing waves have zero amplitude everywhere

B) A point of zero displacement where destructive interference always occurs
C) A point where two waves first meet
D) A point where the wavelength is maximum

A) A point of zero displacement where particles don't move
B) A point of maximum displacement where constructive interference occurs
C) A region where only the reflected wave exists
D) A point where the wave speed is zero
E) The endpoint of a standing wave pattern

B) They must be at nodes (points of zero displacement)

D) They must be at the wavelength center
E) They must move with the wave pattern

How does phase affect whether two waves interfere constructively or destructively?
A) Phase is irrelevant; all waves interfere the same way
B) Waves in phase (crests aligned) create constructive interference; out of phase (crest to trough) create destructive interference
C) Phase only affects the frequency, not interference

E) Phase determines the color of the wave

A) Diffraction
B) Refraction
C) Constructive interference (waves reinforcing each other)
D) Destructive interference (waves canceling each other)
E) Total reflection

A) Constructive interference
B) Refracted interference
C) Specular interference
D) Destructive interference (waves canceling each other)
E) Reflected interference

C) Path difference must equal zero
D) Path difference must be greater than the wavelength
E) Path difference has no effect on interference

For destructive interference to occur in a standing wave pattern, what must be true about the path difference between two waves?

D) Path difference must be less than half a wavelength
E) Path difference must equal one full wavelength
