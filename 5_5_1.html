Sound Wave (Questions 1.1 - 1.3)
1.1
Which of the following best defines a sound wave?
A A transverse wave that travels through the atmosphere
B A longitudinal mechanical wave consisting of pressure variations that travels through matter
C An electromagnetic wave that requires no medium to propagate
D A type of wave that cannot carry energy
E A wave that can only travel through solid materials
1.2
When a tuning fork vibrates, it creates alternating compressions and rarefactions that spread outward through the air. What type of wave is being produced?
A A light wave
B A sound wave
C A mechanical transverse wave
D An electromagnetic wave
E A standing wave
1.3
A speaker produces a wave of pressure variations consisting of compressions and rarefactions that travels through air and causes your eardrum to vibrate. This describes a:
A light phenomenon
B sound wave
C static pressure difference
D mechanical disturbance in a vacuum
E temperature gradient
Pitch (Questions 1.4 - 1.6)
1.4
Pitch is best described as:
A The intensity of sound
B The perceived highness or lowness of a sound
C The speed at which sound travels
D The quality or color of a sound
E The wavelength in meters
1.5
A musician plays a high C note with a frequency of 523 Hz, then plays a low C note with a frequency of 262 Hz. Which statement is correct?
A Both notes have the same pitch
B The low C note has a higher pitch than the high C note
C The high C note has a higher pitch because it has higher frequency
D Pitch depends on the amplitude, not frequency
E The two notes have different loudness but the same pitch
1.6
When you tune a guitar string by tightening it, the frequency of vibration increases. According to the relationship between frequency and pitch, what happens to the pitch of the note produced?
A The pitch becomes lower
B The pitch becomes higher
C The pitch remains constant
D The pitch becomes inaudible
E The pitch depends on the string's color
Loudness (Questions 1.7 - 1.9)
1.7
Loudness is primarily related to which property of a sound wave?
A Frequency
B Wavelength
C Amplitude
D Period
E Speed of sound
1.8
A student measures sound waves from two different sources. The first wave has an amplitude of 2 units, and the second has an amplitude of 8 units. Which statement is true?
A The first sound is louder
B The second sound is louder
C Both sounds have the same loudness
D Loudness cannot be compared without knowing frequency
E The loudness depends on the speed of sound
1.9
A rock concert produces sound with large pressure variations between compressions and rarefactions. This describes a sound with:
A very low frequency
B very high loudness
C very low amplitude
D very low speed
E very short wavelength
Doppler Effect (Questions 1.10 - 1.12)
1.10
The Doppler effect is best described as:
A The decrease in sound intensity with distance
B The change in observed frequency due to relative motion between source and observer
C The bending of sound waves around obstacles
D The absorption of sound by matter
E The reflection of sound from hard surfaces
1.11
An ambulance siren maintains a constant frequency of 1,200 Hz. As the ambulance approaches you, what do you observe about the frequency you hear?
A The frequency you hear stays at 1,200 Hz
B The frequency you hear increases above 1,200 Hz
C The frequency you hear decreases below 1,200 Hz
D The frequency stops changing when the ambulance passes
E The frequency depends only on the siren's color
1.12
When a moving sound source travels away from a stationary observer, the observer measures a lower frequency than the actual frequency emitted by the source. This occurs because the source's motion:
A compresses the sound waves
B stretches out the sound waves
C reduces the amplitude of the waves
D increases the speed of sound
E changes the medium through which sound travels

1.13
Timbre is best defined as:
A The height or depth of a sound
B The loudness of a sound
C The quality or color of a sound that distinguishes different sources
D The speed at which sound travels
E The distance sound travels

A The pitch is different
B The loudness is higher for the violin
C The timbre is different due to different combinations of harmonics
D The speed of sound is different

A Both have identical timbre
B The pure tone has simpler timbre; the complex instrument has richer timbre
C The pure tone is louder
D The pure tone has higher frequency
E The complex instrument has lower pitch

1.16

A Frequencies between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz
B Frequencies below 20 Hz, inaudible to humans
C Frequencies above 20,000 Hz, used in medical imaging
D Very low amplitude waves
E Sound that can only travel through solids
1.17
Ultrasound is sound with frequencies:
A Below 20 Hz
B Between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz (human hearing range)
C Above 20,000 Hz
D That varies with temperature
E That cannot be produced by animals

A Ultrasound is louder than audible sound
B Ultrasound cannot be heard by other animals
C Higher frequencies allow detection of smaller objects
D Ultrasound travels faster than audible sound
E Ultrasound has longer wavelengths
Speed of Sound (Questions 1.19 - 1.21)
1.19
The speed of sound is best described as:
A Constant in all materials at the same temperature
B Dependent on the frequency of the sound
C Dependent on the medium through which it travels and on temperature
D Always 343 m/s regardless of conditions

1.20

A Different frequencies of sound in each medium
B Particle spacing and the strength of particle interactions in each medium
C The amplitude of waves being larger in solids
D The wavelength being longer in water
E Water and steel being unable to carry high frequencies
1.21

A Cold air has higher density
B Colder air molecules move more slowly and cannot transmit pressure changes as quickly
C Sound cannot travel in cold temperatures
D The frequency of sound decreases at lower temperatures
E The wavelength becomes longer in cold air
