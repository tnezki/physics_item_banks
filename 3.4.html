Bank 1 – Vocabulary (Multiple Choice)

A. for every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force

C. objects always move in pairs

B. two forces of equal magnitude and opposite direction acting on different objects
C. any two forces that are opposite
D. gravity and normal force

A. the person's weight
B. gravity
C. the ground pushing forward on the person

A. they are equal
B. they are opposite
C. they act on different objects
D. they occur at different times

A. pushing on the air

C. using friction with space
D. gravity pulling it forward

B. the ball pushes on the wall and the wall pushes on the ball with equal and opposite forces
C. the wall is stationary so no force acts on it
D. only the ball experiences a force

A. always greater
B. always less
C. always equal
D. depends on the object's mass

A. zero force

A. equal in magnitude and opposite in direction
B. greater on the car
C. greater on the truck
D. zero on both
E. unequal
11. Tension in a rope pulling an object is an example of
A. a single force
B. an action force only
C. gravity
D. an action-reaction pair transmitted through the rope
E. friction
12. When a person jumps, they push down on the ground and the ground pushes up on the person. These forces
A. are equal and opposite
B. are unequal
C. cancel out
D. occur at different times
E. both act on the same object
13. A satellite orbits Earth. Which statement correctly describes the forces?
A. Earth pulls on the satellite but the satellite doesn't pull on Earth
B. the satellite pulls on Earth and Earth pulls on the satellite with equal and opposite forces
C. both pull with unequal forces
D. there are no forces between them
E. gravity only acts on the satellite
14. When a hammer hits a nail, the hammer exerts a force on the nail. By Newton's Third Law, the nail
A. doesn't exert a force
B. exerts an equal and opposite force on the hammer
C. exerts a smaller force
D. exerts a force in the same direction
E. exerts a force that depends on the nail's size
15. Newton's Third Law applies
A. only to moving objects
B. only to stationary objects
C. to all objects and all interactions
D. only to objects in space
E. only when friction is present
16. Two objects collide head-on. By Newton's Third Law, the net force on the system of both objects is
A. maximum
B. zero
C. equal to the larger object's force
D. equal to the smaller object's force
E. unpredictable
17. A fish swims forward by pushing water backward. The reaction force is
A. the fish's weight
B. water pushing backward on the fish
C. the water pushing forward on the fish
D. friction
E. gravity
18. When a book rests on a table, the book pushes down with force equal to its weight. The table pushes up on the book with a force that is
A. zero
B. less than the book's weight
C. equal to the book's weight (normal force)
D. greater than the book's weight
E. unpredictable
19. The reason Newton's Third Law forces don't cancel to produce zero net force is that
A. they have different magnitudes
B. they act on different objects
C. they act at different times
D. one is stronger than the other
E. they are not actually equal
20. A baseball bat hits a baseball. The force of the bat on the ball
A. is greater than the force of the ball on the bat
B. is less than the force of the ball on the bat
C. equals the force of the ball on the bat
D. depends on the bat's material

Bank 2 – Conceptual (Multiple Choice)

1. If Newton's Third Law forces are equal and opposite, why do a truck and a car not end up with the same change in velocity when they collide?
A. the forces are not actually equal
B. the truck is stronger
C. equal forces produce different accelerations depending on mass
D. Newton's Third Law doesn't apply to collisions
E. one force occurs before the other

A. Earth is stationary
B. the force is too small to move Earth
C. Earth has such enormous mass that even equal forces produce negligible acceleration
D. Newton's Third Law doesn't apply to Earth
E. gravity prevents Earth from moving

A. the astronaut moves away from the spacecraft at some velocity
B. the spacecraft moves away from the astronaut (reaction)
C. both astronaut and spacecraft move away from each other

E. only the astronaut moves because they're lighter

A. the forces are not really equal

C. the bullet is stronger than the gun
D. Newton's Third Law doesn't apply
E. the gun is fixed in place
5. When you jump, you push down on the ground and the ground pushes up on you. Why do you accelerate upward instead of the ground moving downward?
A. the ground's force is stronger
B. the ground is stationary
C. your mass is much smaller than Earth's, so equal forces cause greater acceleration for you
D. gravity prevents the ground from moving
E. action and reaction forces happen at different times
6. A boat moving forward by pushing water backward. This is an example of
A. action without reaction
B. Newton's Third Law
C. gravity only
D. friction
E. inertia
7. Two identical cars collide head-on. They exert equal and opposite forces on each other. Why do they not simply remain at rest?
A. they do remain at rest

D. one car is heavier

8. When you push against a wall and don't move, what can we conclude?
A. you're not really pushing
B. the wall exerts an equal and opposite force on you, and forces on your body are balanced
C. the wall doesn't push back
D. you would move if the wall were weaker
E. Newton's Laws don't apply to stationary objects
9. In a game of pool, the cue ball hits a stationary ball. After collision, both balls move. This occurs because
A. the cue ball exerts force without receiving reaction force
B. only one ball experiences a force
C. equal and opposite forces cause both balls to move away from each other
D. the stationary ball was already moving
E. gravity caused both balls to move
10. A rope pulls two blocks across a table with the same force throughout. If the rope pulls the first block with 200 N, the tension throughout the rope is
A. 200 N in all parts (assuming massless rope)
B. 100 N
C. zero at some points
D. stronger at one end

A. ice is cold
B. the person is cautious
C. the person wants to distribute forces over a longer time and greater area
D. Newton's Laws don't apply to ice
E. friction increases on ice
12. In a tug-of-war, Team A pulls with 1000 N and Team B pulls with 900 N. The rope accelerates in Team A's direction. Why?
A. both forces are equal
B. the forces are equal, but the rope is connected to both teams, so the net force is not zero
C. Team A's force is greater
D. Newton's Third Law doesn't apply
E. Team B is not really pulling
13. A feather and a hammer fall from the same height on the Moon. They experience different gravitational forces (action-reaction with Moon), yet both fall at the same rate. Why?
A. the feather doesn't experience gravity
B. the forces are equal
C. equal gravitational forces produce equal accelerations only when masses are equal
D. Newton's Third Law doesn't apply on the Moon

14. A person in a boat jumps off. The boat moves backward and the person moves forward. This demonstrates
A. that the person is heavier
B. that the boat is heavier
C. Newton's Third Law; equal forces on different masses produce different accelerations

A. no force on Earth

C. a larger downward force on Earth

Bank 4 – Multi-Step Calculations (Numeric Response)

1. Two ice skaters with masses 65 kg and 50 kg push off each other on a frozen pond. If the 65 kg skater accelerates at $1.5 \text{ m/s}^2$, calculate the force they exert on each other. Then determine the acceleration of the 50 kg skater.
2. A collision occurs between a 1500 kg car and a 3000 kg truck. Both vehicles experience a collision force of 90,000 N. Calculate the acceleration of the car, then the acceleration of the truck. Which vehicle experiences greater acceleration, and why?
3. Two swimmers push off each other in a pool to move apart. The 70 kg swimmer experiences an acceleration of $2.0 \text{ m/s}^2$ while the 80 kg swimmer experiences a different acceleration. Calculate the force they exert on each other. Then determine the 80 kg swimmer's acceleration.
4. During a volleyball competition, a 60 kg athlete jumps on Earth where $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Calculate the person's weight. If the person accelerates upward at $3.0 \text{ m/s}^2$ during the jump, find the normal force from the ground. What is the net force?
5. A tow truck pulls a 2000 kg disabled vehicle using a rope with tension 5000 N. By Newton's Third Law, the car pulls back on the rope with equal force. Calculate the car's acceleration if friction is 800 N. What is the net force on the car?
6. A tennis serve involves a racket hitting a 0.2 kg ball. The racket exerts 1200 N on the ball, and by Newton's Third Law, the ball exerts 1200 N on the racket. Calculate the ball's acceleration. If contact lasts 0.01 seconds, what velocity does the ball reach from rest?
7. A spacecraft launch requires a rocket with mass 50,000 kg to create upward thrust. The engine expels gases downward with a force of 2,500,000 N. With the rocket's weight approximately 490,000 N using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, calculate the net upward force. Then find the rocket's acceleration.
8. An elevator moves a 75 kg person at increased acceleration. The elevator accelerates upward at $2.5 \text{ m/s}^2$. Calculate the person's normal weight using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Then find the apparent weight (normal force) from the elevator floor. What is the net upward force on the person?
9. Two blocks are placed against a wall and released. Block A has mass 4 kg and Block B has mass 6 kg. They push off each other with action-reaction forces of 120 N each. Calculate the acceleration of each block. After 2.0 seconds, determine which block has greater velocity if both start from rest.
10. At an ice skating rink, a 55 kg child and an 85 kg adult stand facing each other on skates. They push off each other with equal forces of 440 N (action-reaction pair). Calculate the acceleration of the child. Then find the acceleration of the adult. Who moves faster and why?
11. A motorcycle accident involves a 900 kg motorcycle and a 2700 kg truck in a head-on collision. Both experience the same collision force of 180,000 N. Calculate the acceleration of the motorcycle. Then calculate the truck's acceleration. Express the ratio of the motorcycle's acceleration to the truck's acceleration.
12. In baseball, a bat collides with a ball in a split second. The 1.2 kg bat exerts 3000 N on a 0.15 kg baseball, and by Newton's Third Law, the ball exerts 3000 N on the bat. Calculate the acceleration of the ball. Then find the acceleration of the bat. Which experiences greater acceleration?
13. A person pushes a box across a floor using action-reaction forces of 210 N each. The 70 kg person pushes the 30 kg box with this force. Friction on the box is 80 N. Calculate the box's acceleration. Then determine the person's acceleration assuming no friction acts on the person. Why do they have different accelerations?
14. During a spacewalk training exercise, an astronaut with total mass 150 kg (including suit) and a spacecraft with mass 5000 kg interact via a 400 N push-off force (action-reaction). Calculate the astronaut's acceleration. Then find the spacecraft's acceleration. After 10 seconds, what are their velocities?
15. A book rests on a table in a lab setting. The 2.5 kg book is on a 5.0 kg table. Calculate the normal force the table exerts on the book using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Now suppose someone accelerates the table upward at $4.0 \text{ m/s}^2$. Calculate the new normal force between book and table. Explain why the normal force increased.
