7. A child with mass 42 kg visits Mars. Find the child's weight on Mars where $g = 3.7 \text{ m/s}^2$.
8. Calculate the weight experienced by a 72 kg astronaut who is standing on Jupiter's surface where $g = 24.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.
9. A bowling ball has a mass of 5.5 kg. What is the weight of this ball on Earth? (Use $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$)
10. Determine the weight of a 68 kg person standing at rest inside an elevator on Earth. (Use $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$)
11. A scale shows that an object experiences a weight of 490 N on Earth. What is the object's mass? (Use $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$)

14. An astronaut on the Moon's surface has a weight of 136 N. Determine the astronaut's mass given that $g = 1.6 \text{ m/s}^2$.

Bank 4 – Multi-Step Calculations (Numeric Response)

1. A 60 kg person travels to different planets. Calculate the person's weight on Earth using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Then determine what the person would weigh on the Moon where $g = 1.6 \text{ m/s}^2$. What is the ratio of weights?
2. During a space mission, an astronaut's equipment has a mass of 35 kg. Find the weight of the equipment on Earth where $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Now calculate the equipment's weight on Mars where $g = 3.7 \text{ m/s}^2$. How much less does it weigh on Mars?
3. A bathroom scale shows that an object weighs 294 N on Earth. Determine the object's mass using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Then calculate what the same object would weigh on the Moon where $g = 1.6 \text{ m/s}^2$.
4. Engineers need to lift a 50 kg crate on Earth. Calculate the weight of the crate using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Then determine the normal force if the crate were accelerating upward at $2.5 \text{ m/s}^2$.
5. An object with mass 75 kg is being compared across the solar system. Find its weight on Earth using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Calculate what the same object would weigh on Jupiter where $g = 24.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. What is the ratio of Jupiter's weight to Earth's weight?
6. A space agency is launching a probe with mass 420 kg. Calculate its weight on Earth where $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Then find the probe's weight on Mercury where $g = 3.7 \text{ m/s}^2$. By how much more does it weigh on Earth?
7. An astronaut on Earth weighs 784 N, which corresponds to a mass of 80 kg using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. If the astronaut travels to another planet and weighs 585 N, determine the gravitational field strength on that planet.
8. A person with mass 70 kg enters an elevator on Earth. First, calculate the person's actual weight using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Then determine the normal force (apparent weight) if the elevator accelerates upward at $1.5 \text{ m/s}^2$.
9. A 55 kg object rests on a scale on Earth showing a weight of 539 N. Using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, verify the mass. Then calculate what the object would weigh on Venus where $g = 8.9 \text{ m/s}^2$. By how many newtons does the weight decrease?
10. A spacecraft is studying different gravitational environments. A 120 kg astronaut weighs 1176 N on Earth (using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$). Calculate the astronaut's weight on a small moon where $g = 2.0 \text{ m/s}^2$. What fraction of the Earth weight is this?
11. A researcher compares the weights of two objects. One has mass 45 kg and the other has mass 30 kg. Calculate both weights on Earth using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. What is the difference between their weights?
12. During field research, scientists measure an object weighing 784 N on Earth. Using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, determine the object's mass. Then predict what the object would weigh on Saturn where $g = 10.5 \text{ m/s}^2$.
13. A 65 kg hiker begins climbing a mountain at sea level where the weight is 637 N. Using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$, verify this measurement. At the summit (8 km up), the gravitational field strength decreases to $g = 9.78 \text{ m/s}^2$. Calculate the hiker's weight at the summit and find the change.
14. An object weighs 1960 N on Earth's surface. First, find its mass using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Then calculate what this object would weigh on the Moon ($g = 1.6 \text{ m/s}^2$), Mars ($g = 3.7 \text{ m/s}^2$), and Jupiter ($g = 24.8 \text{ m/s}^2$). Which of these celestial bodies results in the smallest weight?
15. A student with mass 40 kg is in an elevator that suddenly accelerates downward at $3.0 \text{ m/s}^2$. Calculate the student's normal weight on Earth using $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Then find the apparent weight (normal force) felt in the descending elevator. What is the difference between these weights?
