Bank 4 – Multi-Step Calculations (Numeric Response)

1. A 15-ohm resistor is initially connected to a 30-volt source. First calculate the initial current. Then, if the voltage is increased to 45 volts (keeping resistance constant), determine the new current. Answer: final current in amperes.
2. You measure a circuit component and find: voltage = 24 V, current = 3.0 A. First calculate the resistance. Then determine what current would flow if you increased the voltage to 36 V while keeping resistance constant. Answer: final current in amperes.
3. A circuit initially has 18 volts across a 9.0-ohm resistor. Calculate the initial current. Then, if a different resistor of 6.0 ohms replaces the original while keeping voltage at 18 V, determine the new current. Answer: final current in amperes.
4. An engineer measures V = 12 V and I = 4.0 A in a circuit. First find the resistance. If the engineer wants to reduce the current to 2.0 A while keeping the same resistor, what voltage should be applied? Answer: new voltage in volts.
5. A 100-ohm resistor initially has 2.0 amperes flowing through it. Calculate the initial voltage. If you want to double the current to 4.0 amperes through the same resistor, what voltage is needed? Answer: new voltage in volts.
6. Calculate the power dissipated by a resistor with 50 ohms of resistance when 6.0 volts is applied across it. First find the current using Ohm's Law, then calculate power using $P = IV$. Answer: power in watts.
7. A device operates at 120 volts and has 30 ohms of resistance. First determine the current. Then calculate how much charge flows through it in 5.0 minutes. Answer: charge in coulombs.
8. A 20-ohm resistor is connected to a 60-volt battery for 10 minutes. First calculate the current. Then determine the total energy dissipated. Answer: energy in kilojoules.
9. You have a 9.0-volt battery and want 0.50 amperes to flow through a circuit. Calculate what resistance you need. Then determine the power dissipated. Answer: power in watts.
10. A variable resistor is adjusted from 40 ohms to 80 ohms while connected to a constant 24-volt source. Calculate the current at the initial 40-ohm setting. Then determine the current at the final 80-ohm setting. Answer: final current in amperes.
11. Three students each measure different circuits. Student A: 10V, 2A. Student B: 15V, 3A. Student C: 20V, 5A. Calculate the resistance for each circuit, then determine which circuit has the highest resistance. Answer: resistance of highest-resistance circuit in ohms.
12. A 12-volt automotive circuit has a 4.0-ohm load. First calculate the current. Then determine how much energy is transferred if this current flows for 30 seconds. Answer: energy in joules.
13. An LED requires exactly 0.020 amperes (20 milliamps) to operate properly. If you have a 9.0-volt battery, first calculate what resistance you need in series to limit the LED current to 0.020 A. Then calculate the power dissipated by that resistor. Answer: power in milliwatts.
14. A heating element has 15 ohms of resistance. When connected to a power source, it draws 8.0 amperes. First determine the voltage of the source. Then calculate the power consumed by the heater. Answer: power in watts.
15. A technician measures a component and finds R = 25 ohms. The component is rated to handle a maximum power of 100 watts. Determine the maximum current that can safely flow through it using $P = I^2R$. Answer: maximum current in amperes.
16. Calculate what happens in this scenario: Initial circuit has V = 18 V, R = 12 ohms. Find initial current. Then both voltage and resistance are cut in half (V = 9V, R = 6 ohms). Calculate the new current. Answer: final current in amperes.
17. A wire with resistance 0.50 ohms carries 20 amperes. First calculate the voltage drop across the wire. Then determine how much power is wasted as heat in the wire. Answer: power in watts.
18. An electric kettle draws 12 amperes from a 240-volt outlet. First calculate its resistance. Then determine how long it takes to transfer 1.5 MJ (1,500,000 joules) of energy. Answer: time in seconds.
19. A circuit has two possible voltage settings: 6.0 V and 12 V. When using 6.0 V, the current is measured as 1.5 A. Calculate the circuit resistance. Then predict the current when switched to 12 V. Answer: current at 12 V in amperes.
20. A 100-watt lightbulb operates at 120 volts. First calculate the current it draws. Then determine its resistance using Ohm's Law. Answer: resistance in ohms.
21. A solar panel outputs 18 volts and can deliver a maximum of 5.0 amperes. Calculate the minimum load resistance needed to draw maximum current. Then determine the maximum power output. Answer: maximum power in watts.
22. A circuit initially operates with V = 50 V and R = 25 ohms. Calculate the initial current. If you want to keep the same current but reduce voltage to 30 V, what should the new resistance be? Answer: new resistance in ohms.
